🦕 The Triassic Period

252 – 201 million years ago

The Triassic Period marks the beginning of the Mesozoic Era — often called the “Age of Reptiles.” It lasted about 51 million years, from 252 million years ago (mya) to 201 mya. It was a time of dramatic recovery, new beginnings, and evolutionary breakthroughs after Earth’s worst mass extinction.

252–247
mya

🌋 Early Triassic

The Great Dying (Permian-Triassic Extinction)

At the start of the Triassic, Earth was nearly lifeless. Around 90–95% of marine species and 70% of land vertebrates had been wiped out in the extinction event 252 mya, likely caused by massive volcanic eruptions in Siberia, which released toxic gases, triggered global warming, and acidified the oceans.

🌋 Early Triassic

Climate & Geography

The supercontinent Pangaea stretched from pole to pole, surrounded by the vast Panthalassa Ocean. Much of its interior was hot, dry desert, while coastal regions had seasonal rainfall. The poles were warm and lacked permanent ice caps.

🌋 Early Triassic

Life on Land:

Recovery was slow. The early Triassic was dominated by hardy survivors like lycophytes (club mosses) and glossopterids in some southern regions. Amphibians and a few reptiles survived, but ecosystems were unstable.

🌋 Early Triassic

Life in the Seas

Marine ecosystems were devastated. Coral reefs had collapsed. Slowly, new groups emerged,Ammonoids (relatives of squids) began to diversify again. Ichthyosaurs, early dolphin-like reptiles, appeared and quickly adapted as top marine predators. Small fish and bivalves (clams, mussels) became more common.

247–237
mya

🌿 Middle Triassic

Environmental Recovery

By the middle Triassic, climates stabilized. Rainfall increased in some regions, giving rise to more forests along coastal zones. Deserts still dominated the interior of Pangaea, but ecosystems were more balanced.

🌿 Middle Triassic

Marine Life

Oceans flourished again. The first modern-style coral reefs were built by scleractinian corals. Large marine reptiles, including nothosaurs and placodonts, joined ichthyosaurs in ruling the seas.

🌿 Middle Triassic

Land Plants

Vegetation expanded with conifers, cycads, ginkgoes, and ferns dominating landscapes. These became the staple diets for large herbivorous reptiles.

🌿 Middle Triassic

Land Animals

The archosaurs (a group including ancestors of crocodiles, pterosaurs, and dinosaurs) began to dominate.Therapsids (mammal-like reptiles) still survived but were shrinking in diversity. The first turtles appeared in this period.

🌿 Middle Triassic

Flying Reptiles

By the late Middle Triassic (~240 mya), the first pterosaurs evolved — the earliest vertebrates capable of powered flight.

237–201
mya

🦖 Late Triassic

Continental Drift & Volcanism

Pangaea began to rift slowly, especially in the region that would become the Atlantic Ocean. Volcanic activity increased, shaping the climate.

🦖 Late Triassic

The First Dinosaurs (~230 mya)

In South America, small bipedal dinosaurs such as Eoraptor and Herrerasaurus appeared. Though not yet dominant, they were swift, agile, and well-adapted to the dry climate. These were the ancestors of the giant dinosaurs that would soon dominate the Jurassic.

🦖 Late Triassic

Rise of Mammals (~225 mya)

From small cynodonts (advanced mammal-like reptiles), the first true mammals evolved. They were tiny, nocturnal insect-eaters — overshadowed by reptiles but crucial for future evolution.

🦖 Late Triassic

Flourishing Ecosystems

Conifer forests became widespread as the large reptiles such as phytosaurs (crocodile-like creatures) thrived in rivers and lakes. Giant amphibians still lived in swamps. Herbivorous reptiles like Plateosaurus roamed the land in herds.

🦖 Late Triassic

Marine World

Marine reptiles like plesiosaurs (long-necked hunters) appeared late in the Triassic, alongside ichthyosaurs and nothosaurs. Oceans were full of ammonites, fish, and early sharks.

201
mya

🌍 End-Triassic Extinction

~201 million years ago

The Triassic ended with another mass extinction, one of the “Big Five." Likely caused by massive volcanic eruptions in the Central Atlantic Magmatic Province, which released carbon dioxide and sulfur, triggering climate chaos, acid rain, and ocean acidification. Many large amphibians, marine reptiles, and non-dinosaur archosaurs went extinct. Dinosaurs survived and were left as the dominant land vertebrates, setting the stage for the Jurassic Period.
